# A naturalist's note on the particles of Primordia

At first glance the inhabitants of Primordia look like bright motes in a
shallow dish: specks, swirls, little eddies of color sliding through broth.
That first impression is misleading. Each particle is a living experiment: a
body with energy, senses, inherited tendencies, a mutable brain, and a small
repertoire of acts that can become surprisingly consequential when multiplied
across generations.

They are not puppets. They are born with genomes, act through neural controllers,
eat, signal, collide, bond, reproduce, mutate, and die. The world does not tell
them what a nest, trap, pack, road, shelter, or ambush is. It gives them food,
danger, materials, neighbors, signals, and time. If higher behavior appears, it
has to assemble itself from those parts.

## Bodies and energy

A particle has a position, velocity, species identity, age, energy reserve, and
genome. Energy is the immediate currency of life. Particles gain it mainly by
eating food from the local chemical field. They lose it through baseline
metabolism, movement and ecological pressure, mud traversal, signaling costs,
wall carrying, construction, injury, reproduction, and other burdens.

When energy falls to zero, a particle dies. Death is not just deletion. It feeds
the world by depositing decay, which can later become food again. If a particle
dies while carrying wall material, it may drop a solid wall block into the world
with ownership metadata attached. A dead particle may also become a fossil record
if it was sufficiently notable.

## Senses

Particles do not see the whole world. Their awareness is local, partial, and
filtered through evolved sensors.

They can sense:

- their own energy and age
- local food and decay concentration
- food and decay gradients
- nearby own-species and other-species particles
- average nearby velocity
- nearby visual signal color
- sound fields
- bond messages from bonded partners
- rough quadrant counts and signal levels
- cluster centroid, size, membership, and alarm state
- whether they are carrying wall material
- nearby terrain in cardinal directions:
  - any wall/material
  - mud
  - solid wall
  - glass
- whether they are currently standing in mud

Terrain transmission matters. Solid walls block movement, chemistry, sound, and
direct particle/signal perception. Glass blocks movement but transmits chemistry,
sound, and sight-like signal perception. Mud slows and drains bodies passing
through it, but also transmits chemistry, sound, and sight-like signal
perception.

Thus a particle may be able to sense food, sound, or another particle beyond
glass or mud, while still being physically unable to move straight toward it.
This is one of the raw ingredients for obstacle navigation.

## Movement and contact

Particles move through a continuous 2D world over a grid of fields and terrain.
Their motion is shaped by:

- repulsion at very close range
- attraction or aversion toward other species
- same-species cohesion
- food and decay gradient forces
- neural thrust outputs
- collision with solid and glass walls
- slowing and energy drain inside mud
- bond spring forces
- bond barriers formed by named clusters

Their social physics is not symmetric in the human sense. A genome can evolve to
seek one species, avoid another, cling to kin, chase prey, or become broadly
social. The attraction matrix panel is a useful field guide to these relations:
rows are emitters, columns are targets, blue means attraction, red means
repulsion.

## Brains and inheritance

Each particle has a small recurrent neural controller. It receives the sensor
stream and produces action outputs. The brain can change over evolutionary time:
weights mutate, structure can drift, and hidden capacity can rise within the
current cap.

Reproduction copies and mutates genomes. Some reproduction is solo, some is
sexual crossover when compatible nearby particles are willing. Mutagen fields
increase mutation pressure. Selection is indirect: particles that keep enough
energy and reproduce pass their organization forward.

The important thing to remember is that an individual particle is not simply a
dot with a scripted rule. It is a tiny inherited control system embedded in an
ecology.

## Food, decay, and the closed dish

Food and decay are chemical fields. Particles eat food from their current cell.
Death deposits decay. Decay slowly becomes food. Food can also be painted by the
user or shaped by templates and terrain.

This makes the world less like an arena and more like a dish with recycling
nutrients. A predator boom, a wall-building lineage, or a crowded mud field can
alter what food is reachable and where future generations can survive.

## Signals and sound

Particles emit visual RGB signals and sound-channel events. These are not merely
decoration. They are also sensed by other particles and can feed into brains.
Signal intensity can be costly, so communication that persists should ideally
earn its keep.

Bonded particles also exchange bond messages. These are local, continuous
channels heard through direct bonds. A long chain relays information slowly; a
mesh or hub can change who hears what. Named clusters also have an alarm signal
for urgent broadcast. This creates the possibility of primitive nervous systems:
some members sense stress or opportunity, others receive the message and act.

The music is the audible shadow of this signaling ecology. It is not a fixed
song laid over the simulation; it is driven by organism activity. Species,
actions, energy, death, digging, depositing, and signal events can shape what is
heard. The goal is for the sound to become listenable evidence of ecology, not
just bleeps.

## Bonds and clusters

Particles can bond when close enough and mutually willing. A bond is physical,
energetic, and informational:

- it connects bodies with spring-like force
- it enables energy sharing
- it lets bond messages pass between partners
- it can help named clusters act as larger units
- dense named clusters can form barriers to outsiders

Once a bonded group becomes large and stable enough, it may be named as a
cluster. These clusters are the nearest thing Primordia has to organisms above
the particle level. They can have membranes drawn around them, aggregate stats,
chase controls, export data, and identities such as taxonomic trait/color names
with a human-name suffix.

The open biological question is whether clusters can become meaningfully
multicellular: one region sensing food, another relaying alarm, another digging,
another locomoting, another protecting the group.

## Construction and material handling

Particles can dig solid walls and carry wall material. They can later deposit
solid wall blocks. Digging and depositing cost energy. Carrying material also
has an ongoing cost. Higher-energy particles can carry more than one block, up
to the current cap.

Walls are not anonymous. Deposited walls remember who built them, which cluster
that builder belonged to if any, the clade, and the tick of deposition. The user
can inspect wall segments to see this history.

Construction is deliberately not "build a house" as a scripted task. The world
provides materials, costs, shelter payoffs, barriers, prey, food, and danger.
If particles learn to dig passages, build refuges, trap prey, isolate colonies,
or shape food access, that behavior should emerge from those incentives.

## Death, fossils, and lineages

Death is part of the world cycle. It alters the chemistry, can drop carried wall
material, breaks bonds, and may leave behind a fossil record. The event log and
fossil panel are useful for tracking unusual longevity or lineage success.

Clades track evolutionary descent and complexity. The top clades panel is a
field notebook for what lineages are currently succeeding and why: brain
complexity, communication, construction, diversity, depth, and other measures.

## What to watch for

A naturalist in Primordia should watch less for one heroic particle and more for
patterns:

- do particles turn toward food gradients?
- do some lineages tolerate mud while others avoid it?
- do clusters survive longer than lone particles?
- do bonded groups coordinate wall digging or depositing?
- do signals precede movement, feeding, defense, or construction?
- do glass barriers create visible frustration, detours, or specialization?
- do walls become shelters, traps, corridors, or accidental fossils of extinct
  builders?
- do top clades become more complex without collapsing the ecosystem?

The promise of Primordia is not that any one of these must happen on command.
It is that the world is being equipped so such things can happen, be measured,
and be selected when they do.
